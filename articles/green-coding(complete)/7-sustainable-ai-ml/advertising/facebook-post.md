## Post

Training GPT-3 emitted 552 tons of CO₂. That's like driving 5 cars for their entire lifetime. 🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗

We're in an AI boom. Every company is training models. ChatGPT serves millions of requests per day. Each request burns compute. Each training run burns more.

Here's what nobody talks about: AI has a massive carbon problem. And it's getting worse as models get bigger.

But it doesn't have to be this way. You can train and deploy ML models efficiently. You just need to be intentional about it.

**Model Efficiency Techniques**:
• Pruning: 90% smaller models, 95% accuracy retained (BERT → 11M parameters)
• Quantization: FP32 → INT8 = 4x speedup, 1-2% accuracy loss
• Knowledge distillation: DistilBERT is 40% smaller, 60% faster, 97% performance
• Use pre-trained models: 100x less compute than training from scratch

**Training Optimization**:
• Mixed precision: 2x faster training with 3 lines of code
• Early stopping: save 20-40% of training time
• Efficient batch sizes: find the sweet spot for your hardware
• Learning rate schedules: converge 30-50% faster

**Carbon-Aware Training**:
• Schedule during low-carbon hours (Microsoft achieved 16% reduction)
• Use renewable-heavy regions (Stockholm: 13 gCO₂/kWh vs Virginia: 400 gCO₂/kWh)
• Pause/resume training based on grid carbon intensity
• Same compute, fraction of the carbon

**Inference Optimization**:
• Batch inference: 10-100x more efficient than single-item
• Dynamic batching: add 10ms latency for 10-100x throughput
• Model caching: 80% cache hit rate = 80% less compute
• Edge vs cloud: choose based on model size and frequency

Real example: A team was retraining their model weekly. When we looked at performance metrics, the model was stable for months. Switched to performance-based retraining (only retrain when accuracy drops). Result: 80% reduction in retraining compute. Same model performance, fraction of the energy.

The key insight: 95% accuracy with 10x efficiency is often better than 98% accuracy with 1x efficiency. You're not building GPT-4. You're building a sentiment classifier. 95% is probably good enough.
