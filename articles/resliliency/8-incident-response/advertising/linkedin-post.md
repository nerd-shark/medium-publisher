The industry average Mean Time To Recovery is 197 minutes. Google and Netflix resolve most incidents in under 10.

Process and preparation close most of that gap — more than raw talent does. What happens in the first 10 minutes usually decides the outcome.

I just published Part 8 of my Resilience Engineering series. It's focused on those opening minutes — the ones that shape whether an incident stays a 30-minute blip or stretches into a 4-hour catastrophe.

Here's what it covers:

A minute-by-minute playbook for the first 10 minutes. Breaking the bystander effect with a single Slack message, triaging severity, and executing your first mitigation attempt before you even understand root cause.

The Incident Commander anti-pattern. The moment the IC opens a terminal and starts debugging, nobody is coordinating. I've watched this stretch 20-minute fixes into three-hour ordeals.

A severity framework you agree on before the fire starts, so you're not debating SEV1 vs. SEV2 while the system is burning. Plus runbook design for 3 AM brains operating at 60% capacity — step-by-step procedures, not vague "investigate and resolve" wish lists.

Blameless post-incident reviews that produce change. Contributing factors instead of root cause, because incidents rarely have a single point of failure. The Navy figured this out decades ago with ship collisions.

And how to build the muscle — game days, tabletop exercises, Google's Wheel of Misfortune — so your response is rehearsed.

Includes runbook templates, a severity matrix, communication templates, and a 4-week implementation plan.
