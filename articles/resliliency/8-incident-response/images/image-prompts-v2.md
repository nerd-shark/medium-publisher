# Featured Image Prompts v2 — Incident Response (Fire Team Metaphor)

## Why v2
The v1 prompts were abstract digital scenes — holographic war rooms, floating runbooks, timeline infographics. They looked like every other "incident response" stock image. The fire team metaphor maps directly to the article's core message: structured roles, coordinated response under pressure, the first minutes deciding the outcome.

---

## Prompt 1: The Engine Company

A fire crew of four in full turnout gear arriving at a commercial building with smoke pouring from the upper floors. The scene is shot from a low angle, looking up at the crew. The captain (incident commander) stands slightly forward, arm raised, directing the team — clearly coordinating, not holding a hose. One firefighter is connecting to a hydrant (technical lead, attacking the problem). Another is on a radio (communications lead, relaying status). A fourth is checking the building's Knox Box for access keys (triage — what changed, what do we know). The building's fire alarm panel is visible through the glass lobby doors, flashing red. Early morning light, pre-dawn blue sky. The crew is calm and methodical despite the smoke — this is rehearsed, not improvised. Style: photorealistic, cinematic lighting, low-angle hero shot. Warm firelight from the building contrasting with cool pre-dawn blue. 16:9 landscape. No text.

## Prompt 2: The First Due

A single fire engine arriving at a structure fire, shot from behind the truck looking toward the building. The officer (IC) is stepping off the rig, looking up at the building, sizing up the situation — the moment of triage before any action. Smoke is showing from two windows on the second floor. The rest of the crew is still on the truck, waiting for direction. The officer's posture is deliberate — assessing, not panicking. Dashboard-mounted instruments glow inside the cab. The scene captures that critical first minute: someone has arrived, someone is in charge, and the response is about to begin. Style: photorealistic, dramatic, shot from behind/beside the engine. Smoke and early morning light. The officer is the focal point. 16:9 landscape. No text.

## Prompt 3: The Command Board

A fire incident commander standing at a portable command board set up on the hood of a battalion chief's SUV, parked across the street from a working fire. The board has magnetic tags representing crews and their assignments — accountability tracking in real time. The IC is on a radio with one hand, moving a tag with the other. Behind the IC, the fire is visible but the IC's back is to it — they're managing the response, not fighting the fire. Two runners (junior firefighters) are approaching with updates. The scene is organized chaos: the fire is serious, but the command post is methodical. Style: photorealistic, medium shot focused on the IC and the command board. Fire glow in the background, command post lit by vehicle headlights and a portable light tower. 16:9 landscape. No text.

## Prompt 4: The Shift Change Briefing

Inside a fire station, a crew of six sitting around a large table during a morning shift briefing. A whiteboard behind them shows the day's assignments, apparatus checks, and a list of recent incidents with notes. The captain stands at the head of the table, pointing at the whiteboard. On the table: coffee mugs, a printed runbook binder open to a procedure page, and a portable radio. The crew is attentive but relaxed — this is practice, not an emergency. One firefighter is taking notes. The station's engine is visible through an open bay door in the background, ready to go. The mood is preparation: building the muscle memory before the alarm sounds. Style: photorealistic, warm interior lighting, documentary feel. The whiteboard and runbook binder are prominent but not readable. 16:9 landscape. No text.

## Prompt 5: The Two-In, Two-Out

A tight shot of a fire crew executing a coordinated entry. Two firefighters are entering a smoke-filled doorway with a charged hose line, crouched low. Two more are positioned just outside the door — the safety team, ready to go in if something goes wrong. A fifth person (the IC) stands back, watching the entry point, radio in hand. The composition emphasizes the structure: paired teams, clear roles, nobody freelancing. Smoke rolls out of the doorway above the entering crew. The safety team's posture mirrors the entry team's — ready, not idle. Style: photorealistic, dramatic, tight framing on the doorway. Smoke and interior firelight create depth. The IC is slightly out of focus in the background but clearly present and watching. 16:9 landscape. No text.

## Prompt 6: The After-Action

A fire crew sitting in a loose circle on the tailboard and running boards of their engine, still in turnout gear with helmets off, doing an informal debrief after a call. The building behind them is intact but shows smoke staining around the windows — the fire is out. One person is talking, gesturing. Others are listening, a couple nodding. One firefighter is writing in a small notebook. The mood is reflective, not celebratory — they're reviewing what happened while it's fresh. An empty coffee thermos sits on the bumper. Late morning light. Style: photorealistic, candid documentary feel, warm natural lighting. The crew and their conversation are the focal point, the building is background context. 16:9 landscape. No text.

## Usage Notes
- All prompts 16:9 landscape for Medium featured images
- No text, no labels, no readable writing on boards/runbooks
- The fire team metaphor maps to the article's structure:
  - Prompt 1 (Engine Company) = The full response with all three roles visible
  - Prompt 2 (First Due) = The critical first minute — triage and acknowledgment
  - Prompt 3 (Command Board) = The IC role — coordinating, not fighting the fire
  - Prompt 4 (Shift Change Briefing) = Building the muscle — practice and runbooks
  - Prompt 5 (Two-In, Two-Out) = Structured roles and safety protocols
  - Prompt 6 (After-Action) = Blameless post-incident review
- Recommended for featured image: Prompt 1 (Engine Company) or Prompt 3 (Command Board)
  - Prompt 1 is the strongest hero shot — all roles visible, immediate visual storytelling
  - Prompt 3 is more nuanced — the IC's back to the fire is a powerful visual for "coordinator, not hero"
- Prompt 6 (After-Action) would make a great secondary image for the post-incident review section
