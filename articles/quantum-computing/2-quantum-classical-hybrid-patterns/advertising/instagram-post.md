## Caption

⚛️ Every quantum computing breakthrough you've read about has one thing in common: a classical computer did most of the work.

The quantum processor? It handled one specific mathematical subroutine. Everything else — data loading, optimization, result interpretation, business logic — stayed classical. Forever.

This is the variational hybrid loop. And here's the thing that clicked for me: it's structurally identical to training a machine learning model.

Classical optimizer picks parameters → quantum circuit runs → measurements come back → cost function evaluates → optimizer adjusts → repeat.

The QPU is a coprocessor. Like a GPU, but for a very different class of problems.

Two algorithms dominate this pattern right now:

🧬 VQE — Variational Quantum Eigensolver
Simulates molecules. The quantum circuit represents a molecular wavefunction, and the classical optimizer finds the lowest energy state. Drug discovery, materials science, catalyst design.

📊 QAOA — Quantum Approximate Optimization
Solves combinatorial problems. Portfolio optimization, logistics routing, scheduling. Maps constraints onto quantum gates.

The honest take: for most problems at today's scale, classical computers still win. The crossover point is coming, but it's not here yet for production workloads. I included the actual crossover tables so you can see where the research stands.

I also compared four frameworks (Qiskit, Cirq, PennyLane, Braket) from an architect's perspective — not a physicist's.

The article ends with a Monday morning checklist. One afternoon to get a VQE simulation running on a real quantum processor through the cloud. No physics degree required.
