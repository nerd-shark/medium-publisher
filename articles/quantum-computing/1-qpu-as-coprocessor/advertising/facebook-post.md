If you watched GPUs go from gaming hardware to the backbone of machine learning in the 2010s, the quantum processing unit trajectory looks familiar.

A QPU is a coprocessor — specialized hardware that sits alongside your classical compute for specific problem types. You won't run your database on one. But for combinatorial optimization, molecular simulation, and certain ML tasks, a QPU can explore solution spaces that would take classical hardware longer than you're willing to wait.

New article covers what's actually inside a quantum computer (and why the 15 millikelvin operating temperature matters for your architecture), how you access QPUs today through cloud APIs, what they cost ($1.60/sec on IBM, $30-100/job on Braket), and the noise problem that defines the current NISQ era.

The architectural pattern: your app identifies a quantum-suitable subproblem, ships it to a cloud QPU, gets back a probability distribution, and continues classical execution. It's a function call, not a platform migration.
