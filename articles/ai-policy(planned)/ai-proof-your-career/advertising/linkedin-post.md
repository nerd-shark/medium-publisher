I build AI systems for a living. I'm also watching those systems hollow out careers in real time. Including in my own field.

Software developer postings are down 35% from five years ago. Entry-level tech hiring dropped more than 50%. Bootcamps keep shutting down — Kenzie, Code Fellows, Codeup, then 2U went bankrupt and took university-branded bootcamps with it. 59,000 tech jobs cut in Q1 2026. Senior dev base pay down 10% YoY.

"Learn to code" was the safe career advice for a decade. Didn't age well.

The thing people miss about AI and jobs is that nobody gets fired by AI on a Tuesday. What actually happens is quieter — AI picks off your tasks one at a time until the role is mostly hollow. The title survives. The work underneath it is different. McKinsey found current AI could automate 57% of US work hours with today's technology, not some future version of it.

Shopify's CEO now requires teams to prove AI can't do the work before requesting headcount. GitHub's study showed developers finish tasks 55% faster with Copilot. Salesforce said AI handles 30-50% of their work. The math on headcount isn't complicated.

So the useful question isn't "will AI take my job" — it's which of your tasks AI will absorb in the next couple years, and what's left when it does.

I wrote up a framework for working through that. It starts with a task audit — list everything you do in a week, score each task honestly on whether AI could handle it 80% as well. The gap between what people expect and what they find is usually pretty wide.

What I keep coming back to: position yourself next to AI, not in competition with it. The person directing AI output and deciding what to do with it has a longer runway than the person doing what AI already does well. Domain expertise combined with judgment — reading a room, knowing which rules actually bend, catching what the model missed — that's the moat. It doesn't live in any training dataset.

Beyond that: expect 3-5 career transitions, not one reskilling moment. Skill portability beats credential prestige. Financial flexibility is career flexibility — six months of savings is what lets you wait for a good move instead of taking a desperate one. And skip the prompt engineering course. Two years of shelf life, maybe.

I tried to be honest about where individual advice runs out, too. Career flexibility correlates with existing privilege. The article doesn't pretend otherwise.
