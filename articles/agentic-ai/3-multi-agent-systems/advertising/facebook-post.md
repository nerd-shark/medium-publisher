🤖 The Jack-of-All-Trades Problem in AI

A skilled carpenter building a house alone is impressive. But will the electrical work pass inspection? What about the plumbing in five years?

This is exactly the challenge with single AI agents. They can do remarkable things, but they're fundamentally limited by what one "person" can accomplish.

The real power emerges when multiple specialized agents work together—just like a construction crew where each expert brings deep knowledge that a generalist simply can't match.

---

**REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE: CUSTOMER SERVICE MULTI-AGENT SYSTEM**

Five specialized agents working together:
• Triage Agent: Classifies issues (94% accuracy)
• Knowledge Agent: Searches documentation (200ms response)
• Resolution Agent: Crafts responses (800ms generation)
• Escalation Agent: Decides human handoff (85% confidence threshold)
• Learning Agent: Improves the system continuously

**Results from telecom/e-commerce deployments:**
✅ 65% of interactions handled autonomously
✅ 12-second average response time (vs 4 minutes with humans)
✅ 60% reduction in support costs
✅ 4.2/5 customer satisfaction scores

---

**THE COORDINATION CHALLENGE**

Here's the interesting part: multi-agent systems don't always have a "boss." Sometimes they negotiate. Sometimes they compete. Sometimes they just figure it out as they go.

Three coordination patterns:
1. **Hierarchical (boss-worker)** - 80% of production systems use this because it's easier to debug
2. **Peer-to-peer (collaborative)** - More common in research than production
3. **Market-based (auction)** - Elegant in theory, rare in practice

---

**THE SWEET SPOT: 3-5 AGENTS**

More agents doesn't mean better results:
• 2 agents: Simple coordination
• 3-5 agents: Good balance (optimal)
• 5-7 agents: Complex but tractable
• 10+ agents: Coordination nightmare

After 5 agents, coordination overhead starts to dominate execution time.

---

**THE ECONOMICS**

Multi-agent systems cost 5x more per request ($0.10 vs $0.02) but achieve 7% higher accuracy (92% vs 85%).

If errors cost $50 each (customer churn, rework):
• Single agent: $7.50 expected cost per interaction
• Multi-agent: $4.00 expected cost per interaction
• Net benefit: $3.42 per interaction

At 100,000 interactions/month, that's $342,000/month in value.

But if errors only cost $5? ROI drops to $2,700/month. The numbers matter.
