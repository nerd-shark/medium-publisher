## Post Content

🤖 What Actually Happens When You Deploy AI Agents to Replace a Back-Office Team

Everyone shares the success metrics. Nobody shares the failures. Here's both.

A 47-person claims processing team deployed a multi-agent AI system. Four specialized agents handling document ingestion, coverage analysis, damage assessment, and compliance checking. An orchestrator coordinating the whole thing.

**The success story (real numbers):**
✅ 83% of workload automated
✅ Processing time: 3.2 hours → 4.2 minutes (97% reduction)
✅ 96.3% accuracy
✅ $2M annual savings
✅ 10.8 month payback
✅ Backlog eliminated (was growing 800 claims/week)

Impressive, right? It is. Genuinely.

---

**Now here's what broke:**

🔴 **Hallucination grounding failures** — The coverage analysis agent occasionally "invented" policy clauses that didn't exist. Grounding checks caught most of them. Not all.

🔴 **Model drift nobody noticed** — Accuracy degraded 3% over six weeks. Nobody caught it because the monitoring dashboards showed aggregate metrics, not per-category breakdowns. The drift was concentrated in one claim type.

🔴 **The expertise drain** — When AI handles 83% of routine work, senior processors only see hard cases. They get sharper on edge cases but lose baseline intuition. Worse: new hires can't learn from routine work that no longer exists. How do you train the next generation?

🔴 **Trust calibration** — Some reviewers rubber-stamped everything the AI produced. Others second-guessed every decision, adding hours of unnecessary review. Neither extreme works.

---

**The economics gap:**

The vendor pitched 70% cost reduction. They got 35%.

Why? Because nobody budgeted for:
• Monitoring infrastructure
• Monthly retraining pipelines
• Human oversight for the 17% edge cases
• Organizational change management
• The new work created by AI (reviewing AI decisions is still work)

35% is still $2M/year. Still a 10.8-month payback. Still worth doing. But it's half what the pitch deck promised.

---

**The organizational impact nobody warns you about:**

The team went from 47 to 19 people. That's 28 people whose roles changed or disappeared. Some moved to AI oversight roles. Some moved to other departments. Some left.

The 19 who remained? Their jobs are harder now. They only see the cases the AI couldn't handle — the ambiguous ones, the judgment calls, the situations requiring empathy. It's more intellectually demanding but also more draining.

---

**The honest assessment:**

Agentic AI in production is transformative but not magical. It requires:
• Continuous monitoring (not just dashboards — active drift detection)
• Regular retraining (models degrade, always)
• Human oversight for edge cases (the 17% matters)
• Change management (people, not just technology)

If you're evaluating AI agent deployments, this case study gives you the real playbook.
