---

2/7

The setup:

• 47-person claims processing team
• 12,000 claims/week
• Backlog growing 800 claims/week
• Training new hires takes 6 months

They built a 4-agent system: document ingestion, coverage analysis, damage assessment, compliance checking.

Result: 83% of workload automated.

---

3/7

The numbers that worked:

• 47 → 19 people
• 3.2 hours → 4.2 minutes per claim
• 96.3% accuracy
• $2M annual savings
• 10.8 month payback

Processing time dropped 97%. The backlog disappeared.

---

4/7

What broke:

• Hallucinations slipped past grounding checks on edge cases
• Model drift: 3% accuracy degradation over 6 weeks — nobody noticed
• Senior processors quit because their jobs got "boring"
• Trust calibration: humans either blindly trusted or constantly second-guessed the AI

---

5/7

The expertise drain problem nobody talks about:

When AI handles 83% of routine work, the remaining humans only see the hard cases. They get better at edge cases but lose touch with the baseline.

New hires can't learn from routine work that no longer exists.

---

6/7

Why 35% instead of 70%:

• Monitoring infrastructure costs
• Retraining pipeline costs
• Human oversight for the 17% edge cases
• Change management nobody budgeted for
• The AI created new work (reviewing AI decisions)

---

7/7

The honest take:

Agentic AI in production works. It also breaks in ways nobody warned you about.

You'll need monitoring that actually catches drift, a retraining pipeline that isn't an afterthought, and people who know when to override the system. Budget for the change management too — that part cost more than the infrastructure.
