## Post Content

A 47-person claims processing team. A multi-agent AI system that now handles 83% of their workload. And the honest truth about what happened next.

This is the series finale of my Agentic AI series — a composite case study drawn from real enterprise deployments. Not the sanitized vendor success story. The real one.

Here's what the numbers actually looked like:

• 47 people → 19 people
• 3.2 hours per claim → 4.2 minutes
• 96.3% accuracy (higher than the human baseline)
• $2M annual savings
• 10.8 month payback period

Sounds like a slam dunk, right? Except the vendor promised 70% cost reduction. They got 35%.

That gap — between what AI vendors promise and what production delivers — is the story nobody tells. The hallucinations that slipped through grounding checks. The model drift that degraded accuracy by 3% over six weeks before anyone noticed. The expertise drain when senior claims processors left because their jobs got "boring." The trust calibration problem where humans either over-relied on the AI or second-guessed everything it did.

The system works. It's genuinely impressive. Four specialized agents — document ingestion, coverage analysis, damage assessment, compliance checking — coordinated by an orchestrator using the Promise/Work pattern. Processing time dropped 97%. The backlog that was growing by 800 claims per week? Gone.

But "replaced a department" is a better headline than reality. They didn't replace the department. They restructured it. The 19 remaining people handle the 17% the AI can't — the ambiguous cases, the judgment calls, the situations where empathy matters more than speed.

The honest assessment: agentic AI in production is transformative but not magical. It requires continuous monitoring, regular retraining, human oversight for edge cases, and organizational change management that nobody budgets for.

If you're evaluating AI agent deployments, this case study gives you the real playbook — what worked, what broke, and what nobody warned us about.
