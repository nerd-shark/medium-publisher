## Thread Version

🧵 Engineering, product, and design speak different languages. Here's how to bridge the gap — from the finale of my Communication series.

1/ Engineers think in systems (constraints, tradeoffs). PMs think in outcomes (metrics, roadmap). Designers think in experience (flows, accessibility). Same meeting, three mental models, three definitions of "done."

2/ The translation gap: "The database can't handle it" → PM hears "engineer is being difficult." Better: "This adds 2 seconds of latency, which reduces conversion by ~8%." Same constraint. Different framing. Now the PM can make an informed tradeoff.

3/ Replace "No" with "Yes, and." Not "we can't do that" → "Yes, and here's how we could approach it within our constraints." You're still communicating the constraint. You're just doing it constructively.

4/ "Can you just add a dropdown?" — the most dangerous phrase. Don't respond with frustration. "Here's what's involved: new API, schema change, frontend component. ~3 days. Or: text input with validation, half a day. Which fits the timeline?"

5/ Always offer options, not ultimatums. "Can't do it by Friday" = ultimatum. "Core flow by Friday, edge cases next sprint" = option. Same info, completely different dynamic.

6/ The engineers who advance fastest multiply their impact through others. Communication is a technical skill. It has patterns and anti-patterns. It responds to practice.
