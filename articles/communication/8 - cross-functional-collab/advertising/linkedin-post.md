A team I worked with spent three months building real-time push notifications. Product identified "engagement" as the target metric. Design created beautiful mockups. Engineering built an impressive WebSocket infrastructure.

Launch day: engagement dropped 12%. Support tickets spiked. Most common complaint: "How do I turn these off?"

Everyone did their job. The product still failed.

Product assumed users wanted notifications because competitors had them. Design polished the wrong thing. Engineering executed without questioning the premise. Three disciplines working in parallel instead of together.

This is the final article in my Communication series — Part 8 of 8.

The core problem: engineering, product, and design speak different languages.

Engineers think in systems — constraints, tradeoffs, technical debt. PMs are tracking outcomes — metrics, roadmap, market timing. And designers are focused on the experience people actually have.

Same meeting, three mental models, three definitions of "done."

What actually works:

→ Translate constraints into business impact. "The database can't handle it" means nothing to a PM. "This adds 2 seconds of latency, which reduces conversion by ~8%" — that lands.

→ "Yes, and" beats "No." Instead of shutting down a request, try framing what's possible within the constraints you're working with.

→ Offer options. "We can ship the core flow by Friday and add edge cases next sprint" is more useful than a flat "can't do that by Friday."

→ Show up to their world. Sit in on user research. Read the product brief before the meeting, not during it.

The engineers who advance fastest aren't always the best coders. They're the ones who multiply their impact through others — by communicating clearly across functions.

I've started treating communication like a technical skill — something with patterns and anti-patterns that gets better with practice.
