# Featured Image Prompts v2 — Cross-Functional Communication

## Why v2
The v1 prompts were abstract metaphors — glowing silhouettes, railroad tracks, floating stones. They could illustrate any article about "collaboration." These prompts show real people in a real project room, with the specific artifacts and body language of cross-functional tension and alignment.

---

## Prompt 1: The Kickoff Table

A wide shot of a conference room during an agile project kickoff meeting. Six people around a large table, clearly from different disciplines — you can tell by what's in front of them. One person has a laptop showing a system architecture diagram (engineering). Another has printed customer survey results spread out, highlighted and annotated (product). A third has a tablet showing wireframes and a color palette (design). A fourth is standing at a whiteboard covered in user story cards and a sprint timeline drawn in marker. The two people at the far end of the table are leaning forward, mid-debate — one gesturing at the whiteboard, the other pointing down at the survey documents, clearly making competing points. The other four are watching, some nodding, one with arms crossed. Post-it notes in multiple colors are stuck to the wall behind the whiteboard — some clustered, some isolated, a few with question marks visible. Coffee cups, water bottles, and a half-eaten box of pastries on the table. The room feels lived-in, mid-morning, natural light from windows on one side. The mood is engaged disagreement — not hostile, but not aligned yet. Style: photorealistic, editorial photography feel, natural office lighting. Shot from a slight elevation (standing height at the doorway). 16:9 landscape. No readable text on post-its or whiteboard.

## Prompt 2: The Whiteboard Standoff

A tighter shot focused on two people standing at a large whiteboard. One person (engineering) has drawn a system flow diagram on the left side — boxes, arrows, database cylinders, API labels. The other person (product) has drawn a customer journey map on the right side — stick figures, emotion curves, decision points. The two diagrams don't connect in the middle — there's a visible gap on the whiteboard between them. Both people are turned toward each other, mid-conversation, each holding a different color marker. Their body language is respectful but firm — this is a real disagreement about approach, not a fight. Behind them, a third person (design) is sitting at the table, looking at both diagrams with a sketchpad open, drawing something that might bridge the two. The wall behind the whiteboard has rows of post-it notes organized in swim lanes. A printed customer survey with pie charts is taped to the wall next to the whiteboard. Style: photorealistic, medium shot, shallow depth of field with the two standing figures sharp and the seated designer slightly soft. Warm office lighting. 16:9 landscape. No readable text.

## Prompt 3: The Post-It Wall

A close-up of a large glass wall covered in post-it notes, shot from inside the conference room looking out. The post-its are organized in columns — different colors representing different disciplines (yellow for product requirements, blue for technical constraints, pink for design considerations, green for user feedback). Some post-its have been moved between columns. A few have arrows drawn between them in dry-erase marker on the glass. One cluster in the center has post-its from all four colors overlapping — the shared priorities. Through the glass, slightly out of focus, you can see the team on the other side — four people standing, looking at the wall from the hallway, one pointing at the center cluster. On the conference table in the foreground, customer survey printouts are spread out alongside a laptop showing analytics dashboards. Style: photorealistic, shot through glass creating depth layers. The post-it wall is the hero, the team is background context. Natural office light. 16:9 landscape. No readable text on post-its.

## Prompt 4: The Sketch Session

Four people huddled around one end of a conference table, all looking down at a large sheet of paper where someone is sketching a user flow in real time with a thick marker. The sketch is rough — boxes, arrows, stick figures, crossed-out alternatives. One person is pointing at a specific part of the sketch, suggesting a change. Another is holding up their phone showing a competitor's app for reference. A third has a printed customer survey open, finger on a specific data point. The whiteboard behind them shows the remnants of an earlier discussion — partially erased diagrams, a few surviving post-it notes. The table has the debris of a working session: marker caps, crumpled post-its, coffee cups, a laptop pushed to the side with Jira visible on screen. The mood is collaborative and focused — they've moved past the disagreement into problem-solving. Style: photorealistic, overhead angle looking down at the sketch and the hands around it. Warm, slightly messy, real working session energy. 16:9 landscape. No readable text.

## Prompt 5: The Two Conversations

A wide shot of a conference room where the meeting has fractured into two separate conversations. On the left side of the table, two people (engineering) are huddled over a laptop, pointing at code or a system diagram, deep in a technical discussion. On the right side, two people (product and design) are looking at printed wireframes and customer survey results, having their own conversation. In the middle, one person (the tech lead or PM) sits between the two groups, looking back and forth, clearly trying to figure out how to reconnect the room. The whiteboard behind them has a half-finished agenda. Post-it notes on the wall. The body language tells the story: the two groups have drifted into their own languages and the person in the middle knows it. Style: photorealistic, wide shot emphasizing the physical gap between the two groups. The person in the middle is the focal point. Natural office lighting, slightly desaturated to emphasize the disconnect. 16:9 landscape. No readable text.

## Prompt 6: The Alignment Moment

A conference room at the end of a productive session. The whiteboard is full — diagrams, user flows, and a timeline drawn across the top, all in multiple marker colors showing different contributors. Post-it notes on the wall have been reorganized into a clear priority order. The team of five is standing together in front of the whiteboard, looking at it — not at each other. One person is taking a photo of the whiteboard with their phone. Another is writing a final post-it and sticking it at the top of the priority column. The table behind them is messy with the evidence of hours of work: survey printouts, sketch paper, empty coffee cups, marker caps everywhere. The mood is satisfied exhaustion — they got there. Late afternoon light coming through the windows, golden hour. Style: photorealistic, shot from behind/beside the group looking at the whiteboard with them. Warm golden light. The whiteboard is the shared artifact they all built together. 16:9 landscape. No readable text.

## Usage Notes
- All prompts 16:9 landscape for Medium featured images
- Real people, real conference rooms, real artifacts — no abstract metaphors
- Key props that ground the scenes: customer survey printouts, whiteboard diagrams, post-it notes in swim lanes, wireframes, system architecture sketches, laptops with dashboards/Jira
- No readable text on any artifacts (avoids distraction and generation artifacts)
- The prompts tell a story arc if used as a set:
  - Prompt 1 (Kickoff Table) = The meeting begins, tension is visible
  - Prompt 2 (Whiteboard Standoff) = Engineering and product see the problem differently
  - Prompt 3 (Post-It Wall) = The artifacts of cross-functional planning
  - Prompt 4 (Sketch Session) = Moving from disagreement to collaborative problem-solving
  - Prompt 5 (Two Conversations) = The anti-pattern — the room fractures
  - Prompt 6 (Alignment Moment) = They got there — shared understanding achieved
- Recommended for featured image: Prompt 1 (Kickoff Table) or Prompt 5 (Two Conversations)
  - Prompt 1 is the safest — immediately readable, shows the full cast and the tension
  - Prompt 5 is more provocative — the fractured room with the person in the middle is a strong visual hook that matches the article's core problem
  - Prompt 6 (Alignment Moment) would make a great closing image if using multiple images in the article
