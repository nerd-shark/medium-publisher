I once spent 3 weeks writing a 47-page RFC for a platform migration. UML diagrams. Risk matrices. Cost projections by quarter.

Two people opened it. One skimmed the conclusion. My skip-level replied: "Can you just give me the highlights on Slack?"

The project got approved the next week. Because I explained it in 5 minutes near the coffee machine.

That RFC was technically perfect and practically useless. Part 6 of my Technical Communication series.

The problem wasn't the technical depth. It was the structure. I wrote it for engineers and sent it to people who aren't engineers.

Your documents have three audiences reading three completely different ways:

Executives skim. Title, first paragraph, conclusion. Maybe a diagram if it doesn't require squinting. They've got 30 seconds and 47 other docs on their desk.

Product managers cherry-pick. Impact, timeline, dependencies. They skip implementation entirely.

Engineers read everything. All of it. They want more.

So you've got one document consumed three different ways. And most of us write the whole thing for that third audience.

The fix isn't three documents. It's layers.

Layer 1: Executive summary. 1 page, 300 words max. Problem, solution, cost, ask. Written last, placed first.

Layer 2: Technical overview. 3-5 pages. Architecture, decisions, tradeoffs. For your engineering director.

Layer 3: Implementation details. Appendix. Unlimited. For the engineers who'll build it.

Each layer stands on its own. Executives stop at Layer 1. PMs stop at Layer 2. Engineers read Layer 3. Nobody wades through stuff that isn't for them.

The real test: hand page 1 to a non-technical colleague. If they can't explain what you're proposing and why — rewrite it.
