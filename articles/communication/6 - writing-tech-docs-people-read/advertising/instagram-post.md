I spent 3 weeks on a 47-page RFC once. UML diagrams. Risk matrices. Cost projections by quarter.

Two people opened it. One skimmed the conclusion. My skip-level replied: "Can you just give me the TL;DR on Slack?"

Project got approved the next week. Because I explained it in 5 minutes near the coffee machine.

That document was technically perfect and practically useless.

The problem: I wrote it for engineers and sent it to people who aren't engineers.

Executives skim. 30 seconds. Title, first paragraph, done.
PMs cherry-pick. Impact, timeline, dependencies.
Engineers read everything. Want more.

One document, three audiences. Most of us write for that third group.

The fix: layers.

Layer 1 — executive summary. 1 page, 300 words. Problem, solution, cost, ask.
Layer 2 — technical overview. 3-5 pages. Architecture and tradeoffs.
Layer 3 — implementation details. Appendix. For the engineers who'll build it.

Each layer stands on its own. Readers stop at their level.

The real test: hand page 1 to a non-technical colleague. If they can't explain your proposal back to you — rewrite it.
