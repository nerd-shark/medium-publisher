October 1, 2013. HealthCare.gov launches. $840 million spent. 55 contractors. 4.7 million visitors on day one.

Six successful enrollments. Six.

Within days: congressional hearings, front-page headlines, "Obama's Katrina."

Then a small team of engineers from Google, Oracle, and Red Hat arrived in a government office building in Maryland. They had 60 days to fix it.

The technical fixes get all the attention. But here's what actually made the rescue work: communication.

In 60 days, that team had to:

→ Brief the President using the Pyramid Principle (Part 1)
→ Navigate stakeholder dynamics — CMS, contractors, the White House, all pulling different directions (Part 2)
→ Present to mixed audiences in daily war rooms (Part 3)
→ Conduct architecture reviews of a system nobody understood (Part 4)
→ Run triage on 200+ critical bugs with competing priorities (Part 5)
→ Write status reports for lawyers and political staff (Part 6)
→ Advocate for fixing technical debt to people who wanted "just fix it" (Part 7)
→ Bridge the cultural gap between Silicon Valley and government (Part 8)

Every communication technique from my 9-part series. Under arguably the most public scrutiny a software project has faced in the U.S.

By December 1: 50,000 concurrent users (up from 1,100). 800,000+ enrollments in December. The rescue team became the United States Digital Service.

The engineers who made it work were technically excellent — and they could communicate under pressure.

This is the capstone of my Communication series — a real case study showing all eight techniques in action.
