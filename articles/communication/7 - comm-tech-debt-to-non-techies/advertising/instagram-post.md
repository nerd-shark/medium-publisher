Watched this happen at a fintech company I was working with.

18 months earlier: new endpoint = 3 days. Velocity: 45 points/sprint. Product loved the team.

By the time I got involved: same endpoint = 2 weeks. Velocity: 28 points. Same team, same people.

VP of Product, every other day: "Why is everything taking so long?"

They didn't need more people. They needed 6 weeks to pay down technical debt. But every time someone said "refactoring" the conversation died.

The problem wasn't that stakeholders didn't care. The engineers were describing it in a language nobody else spoke.

What finally worked — used it at three companies now:

"Remember the shortcut on the payment module for Q3? Saved us 2 weeks. Since then, every payment feature costs 3 extra days. 14 features × 3 days = $84K in engineering time."

"We're paying $84K interest on a $20K loan."

VP's expression changed. He got it immediately. Not because I explained the code. Because I explained the money.

Back it up with real data from your own tools:
- Velocity trending down? Plot it.
- Bug rate climbing? Show the numbers.
- More incidents? Calculate the revenue impact.

Don't ask to refactor. Ask to invest in velocity.

"$120K investment → $200K/year recovered. Payback: 4 months."

Propose 20% of each sprint. Small enough that features keep shipping. Big enough to make real progress.

And celebrate wins loudly. Visible results build trust. Trust gets you more time.
